\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

\subsection{Query Topic Drift Is Pervasive in Real-World LLM Deployments}
\label{sec:motivation-drift}

\noindent\textbf{Observation.}
LLM-powered applications are not deployed into a static world.
User interests shift continuously: new technologies emerge, public events unfold, and cultural trends evolve.
We quantify this phenomenon using two complementary datasets.
On \textbf{WildChat}~\cite{}, a large-scale log of real user--LLM conversations spanning 17 months (April~2023--August~2024), we track the monthly distribution of query topics across eight categories.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:drift}(a), the proportion of \emph{Image Generation} queries grows from 1.2\% in April~2023 to 33.5\% by April~2024 — a \textbf{28$\times$ increase} — while \emph{Coding}-related queries decline from 20.5\% to 14.3\% over the same period.
Monthly Jensen--Shannon divergence (JSD) between consecutive distributions peaks at 0.060, confirming statistically significant and sustained topic drift.
On \textbf{Google Trends}, we track search interest for five AI-application categories (\emph{AI chatbot}, \emph{AI coding}, \emph{AI healthcare}, \emph{AI education}, \emph{AI agent}) from 2022 onward; the maximum month-over-month JSD reaches 0.128, further corroborating that public interest in AI topics shifts dramatically on short timescales.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{motivation/motivation_1/figures/user_query_topic_drift.pdf}
  \caption{Query topic drift observed in real LLM deployments.
    (a)~Monthly topic distribution of WildChat conversations shows rapid compositional shift over 17 months.
    (b)~Google Trends for five AI-application categories exhibits similar non-stationary interest dynamics.}
  \label{fig:drift}
\end{figure}

\noindent\textbf{Implication.}
When the topic distribution of incoming queries shifts away from the knowledge base (KB), retrieval performance degrades --- a phenomenon we term \emph{query--document misalignment}.
This motivates the need for a RAG system that \emph{continuously} monitors and corrects the alignment between user queries and KB contents.

\subsection{Query--Document Misalignment Degrades RAG Performance}
\label{sec:motivation-misalignment}

\noindent\textbf{Empirical evidence.}
To isolate the effect of query--document misalignment, we construct a controlled experiment on the TriviaQA dataset using 20{,}000 questions clustered by topic into seven semantically coherent groups (e.g., \emph{Film Industry}, \emph{Sports History}, \emph{Geography}) via $k$-means on sentence embeddings.
We build five knowledge bases that range from perfectly aligned (uniform topic coverage, JSD$=0.00$) to maximally skewed (one topic dominates~80\% of the KB, JSD$=0.38$).
Results are reported in Figure~\ref{fig:misalignment}.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:misalignment}(b), Hit@10 drops monotonically from \textbf{56.4\%} (aligned) to \textbf{37.9\%} (extreme misalignment) — a relative degradation of \textbf{32.8\%} — while gold-passage coverage falls from 64.2\% to 42.0\%.
Critically, this degradation is \emph{retriever-agnostic}: as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:misalignment}(a), four diverse retrievers (MiniLM-L6 Dense, BGE-Small Dense, BGE-Large Dense, and BGE-Small Hybrid) all suffer a \textbf{$\sim$40\% absolute accuracy drop} upon a domain shift from SQuAD to HotpotQA (JSD $= 0.46$), and all recover to near-original accuracy once aligned documents are progressively added back (+10\% to +100\%).
This confirms that the degradation is a data-level problem, not a model-level one.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{motivation/motivation_2/figures/query_doc_alignment.pdf}
  \caption{Effect of query--document misalignment on RAG performance.
    (a)~Across four retriever architectures, a domain shift causes a $\sim$40\% accuracy drop; progressively adding topic-aligned documents recovers performance.
    (b)~As the KB topic distribution diverges from query distribution (JSD $0\to 0.38$), Hit@10 drops from 56.4\% to 37.9\%.}
  \label{fig:misalignment}
\end{figure}

\noindent\textbf{Key insight.}
\emph{Maintaining alignment between the KB topic distribution and the evolving query distribution is the central challenge in dynamic RAG.}
When alignment is preserved, even a modest-sized KB supports strong retrieval; when it breaks down, retrieval degrades regardless of retriever choice or KB size.

\subsection{Existing Approaches Are Insufficient}
\label{sec:motivation-existing}

The failure mode above exposes a gap that existing ``dynamic RAG'' systems have not addressed.
We categorize prior work into three paradigms:

\noindent\textbf{(1) Full Reconstruction (Naive RAG).}
The simplest strategy rebuilds the entire knowledge base from scratch whenever the query distribution shifts~\cite{}.
While always producing an aligned KB, this approach incurs prohibitive cost: re-encoding millions of documents and rebuilding the index on every update cycle is computationally infeasible for real-time or high-frequency deployments.

\noindent\textbf{(2) Index-Level Incremental Update.}
A large body of work accelerates index maintenance by incrementally inserting or deleting entries in \emph{FAISS}~\cite{johnson2019billion} or graph-based indexes such as HNSW~\cite{malkov2018efficient} and DiskANN~\cite{jayaram2019diskann}.
These methods reduce update latency but operate purely at the \emph{index} level: they assume the document collection is fixed and only optimize data structure maintenance.
Crucially, they do not curate \emph{which} documents should enter or leave the KB based on query relevance, leaving misalignment unaddressed.

\noindent\textbf{(3) Document-Level Update Without Alignment Awareness.}
A third line of work updates the document collection itself, but without targeting query--document alignment:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{Correctness-driven curation} (e.g., ERASE~\cite{}) removes or corrects outdated or erroneous knowledge.
    This improves factual accuracy but does not consider whether the remaining documents match the current query distribution.
  \item \emph{Cache-based augmentation} (e.g., ComRAG~\cite{}) mines high-quality QA pairs from conversational logs and caches them for retrieval.
    This accelerates repeated-query answering but again does not model the evolving topical alignment between queries and the KB.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Gap.}
None of the above approaches explicitly \emph{detects} when query--document misalignment has occurred, nor do they \emph{selectively update} the KB to restore alignment.
As our experiments show, this gap leads to significant and sustained performance degradation.

\subsection{Goals and Technical Challenges}
\label{sec:motivation-challenges}

\noindent\textbf{Goal.}
We aim to build a RAG system that \emph{automatically detects} query--document misalignment and \emph{dynamically curates} the knowledge base to maintain alignment with evolving user query distributions — without requiring full reconstruction or manual intervention.

\noindent\textbf{Challenge~1: Detecting Misalignment Without Ground Truth.}
Unlike correctness verification, query--document alignment has no ground-truth signal: we cannot simply compare KB contents against a reference.
The system must infer whether the \emph{distribution} of incoming queries has diverged from the \emph{distribution} of document topics, using only implicit signals such as retrieval score patterns or coverage statistics.
This requires lightweight, online distribution-shift detection that can run continuously alongside the retrieval pipeline.

\noindent\textbf{Challenge~2: Identifying What to Update.}
Once misalignment is detected, the system must determine \emph{which topics} are over- or under-represented in the KB relative to current query demand.
A coarse global signal (``the KB is misaligned'') is insufficient; selective curation requires a fine-grained topic-level diagnosis that localizes the gap.

\noindent\textbf{Challenge~3: Efficient Targeted Curation.}
Given a diagnosis, the system must \emph{fetch, filter, and integrate} new documents for under-represented topics while \emph{pruning} over-represented ones — all under strict constraints on update latency and storage budget.
Both under-updating (failing to close the gap) and over-updating (flooding the KB and causing new imbalances) must be avoided.

\subsection{Naive Scaling Is Unsustainable}
\label{sec:motivation-scaling-cost}

\noindent\textbf{Observation.}
A natural but inadequate response to query--document misalignment is to \emph{add more documents} indiscriminately, hoping that a larger KB will eventually cover any emerging query topic.
We demonstrate that this strategy is fundamentally unsustainable by benchmarking the retrieval overhead of brute-force dense retrieval (FAISS flat-IP index, 384-dimensional embeddings) as the KB scales from 1K to 100K documents.
Results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-cost}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{motivation/motivation_3/fig_kb_size_vs_latency_memory.pdf}
  \caption{Retrieval overhead grows linearly with KB size under naive scaling.
    (a)~Per-query latency increases \textbf{10.75$\times$} (0.04\,ms $\to$ 0.43\,ms) as the KB grows from 1K to 100K documents.
    (b)~Memory footprint grows linearly from 1.5\,MB to 146.5\,MB (\textbf{100$\times$}).}
  \label{fig:scaling-cost}
\end{figure}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-cost}(a), per-query retrieval latency grows from \textbf{0.04\,ms} at 1K documents to \textbf{0.43\,ms} at 100K documents — a \textbf{10.75$\times$} increase — while the P99 tail latency reaches 0.52\,ms.
Memory consumption, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-cost}(b), scales linearly from \textbf{1.5\,MB} to \textbf{146.5\,MB} (\textbf{100$\times$}), following the expected $O(N \cdot d)$ growth where $N$ is the number of documents and $d = 384$ is the embedding dimension.
Both metrics scale \emph{linearly} with KB size, confirming that naive accumulation cannot be sustained in production deployments where KB size naturally grows over time.

\noindent\textbf{Why existing incremental index methods are not enough.}
While incremental index structures (HNSW, DiskANN) reduce update cost, they do not bound the \emph{size} of the KB — they merely make it cheaper to insert new entries.
As long as documents are added without any principled removal policy, both latency and memory continue to grow without bound.
Moreover, the sub-linear query-time advantage of approximate graph indexes diminishes when documents are added indiscriminately, because a larger, noisier KB degrades retrieval precision alongside speed.

\noindent\textbf{Implication: selective curation is necessary.}
Sustainable RAG requires not only detecting and diagnosing misalignment (\S\ref{sec:motivation-misalignment}), but also maintaining a \emph{bounded, focused} KB through targeted addition of relevant documents and pruning of irrelevant ones.
Indiscriminate scaling is not a viable alternative.
