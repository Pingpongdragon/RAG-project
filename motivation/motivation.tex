\section{Introduction}

Long-running LLM-based agents are expected to improve through interaction, which makes memory a central architectural component rather than a mere prompt extension~\cite{zhang2024survey,hu2025memory,du2026memory}. In the memory-source taxonomy of Zhang et al.~\cite{zhang2024survey}, agents rely on \emph{inside-trial} working memory, \emph{cross-trial} episodic memory, and \emph{external knowledge} retrieved from shared stores such as RAG~\cite{lewis2020rag}. The first two channels are agent-local and are increasingly managed online by systems such as MemGPT~\cite{packer2023memgpt}, MemoryBank~\cite{zhong2023memorybank}, and A-MEM~\cite{weng2025amem}. External knowledge plays a different role: it is often a long-lived shared KB, while many adaptive agents handle freshness or missing-information problems through read-time retrieval and on-demand search~\cite{yan2024crag,asai2023selfrag,vu2023freshllms,kasai2024realtime}. These read-time adaptations can rescue the current interaction, but they do not decide what a budget-limited shared KB should persistently admit, retain, or evict when unsupported needs recur, especially when governance, latency, and storage constraints preclude unlimited online acquisition~\cite{jin2024ragcache,comrag2025,siriwardhana2023improving}.

The missing signal is that user demand itself is non-stationary. User-generated queries naturally change over time based on events in the world and the evolution of language~\cite{yang2025murr}; in multi-user LLM reasoning systems, this appears as a shift in the topic distribution of incoming queries, not merely as a change in the facts available to the system. We term this phenomenon \emph{query distribution drift}. Figure~\ref{fig:motivation0} provides direct empirical evidence in the LLM setting: monthly topic mixtures in WildChat-1M real ChatGPT conversation logs shift measurably from month to month (panel~a), and Google Trends interest for five LLM application domains reweights substantially over a multi-year horizon (panel~b). Together, these signals confirm that persistent shared-KB maintenance is a demand-alignment problem rather than only a freshness problem.

Existing dynamic RAG work does not use this demand signal as a trigger: temporal RAG
refreshes facts when the world state changes~\cite{hoh2025,dynaquest2025}; index-level
methods ingest new documents~\cite{guo2024lightrag,guti2025hipporag2}; editing and
pruning methods reshape an existing corpus~\cite{chen2024recipe,zero-rag2025}; caching
methods favor recent or frequent access~\cite{comrag2025,jin2024ragcache,wu2025streamingrag};
and log-driven refresh waits for delayed answerability or evaluation
feedback~\cite{peng2025uaeval4rag,es2023ragas}. Because none of these triggers on the
immediate signal exposed by unsupported queries, Figure~\ref{fig:motivation1} shows
that under demand shift they consistently update the wrong parts of the shared KB.

Even a deliberately minimal query-driven algorithm, \emph{QueryDriven} (QD), which
accumulates demand over candidate support documents and replaces KB entries only when
repeated demand justifies the write, already substantially outperforms all baselines in
direct-evidence settings (Figure~\ref{fig:motivation1})---where the query usually names
or strongly implies the needed evidence---confirming that query streams carry actionable
maintenance signals. However, in complex multi-hop settings
(Figure~\ref{fig:motivation2}), QD brings only small gains: document replacement driven
by query embeddings is the wrong abstraction when answering requires connecting entities
across a reasoning path, because the query may mention only the first-hop entity while
the reusable evidence lies in second-hop documents. Motivated by these limits, we develop
a query-drift-aware dynamic KB maintenance framework that explicitly detects mismatch
between the incoming query stream and the current KB, then routes toward demand-based
replacement, agent-side acquisition, fetch-to-persist handoff, or graph-structured
evidence expansion as needed.

Across both regimes, our experiments instantiate both abrupt and
gradual demand shifts using standard concept-drift protocols~\cite{gama2014survey}
and recent retrieval-shift analyses~\cite{lupart2023ms,li2024tcr}, by varying the
mixture between head and shifted query distributions either at a single boundary
or over a transition interval.

\textbf{In summary, our main contributions are as follows:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item We formalize \emph{query distribution drift} as a distinct challenge for
    persistent RAG systems under fixed KB budgets, including the shared knowledge
    modules of RAG-augmented agent deployments.
    \item We characterize the simple settings in which query-driven persistence is
    already effective and the more complex settings in which a query-only document
    replacement rule becomes insufficient.
    \item We show that existing static, supply-side, and recency-driven update strategies
    lack explicit query-drift detection and therefore incur mistimed or wasted updates
    under demand shift.
    \item We propose a query-drift-aware dynamic KB maintenance system that explicitly
    detects query drift and routes different regimes toward demand-aware replacement,
    online fetch-and-consolidate, or graph-structured evidence expansion.
\end{itemize}
