\section{Introduction}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has substantially improved large language models (LLMs) on knowledge-intensive tasks~\cite{lewis2020rag}. In deployment, however, the central question is not whether retrieval helps in general, but how a system should respond when its current KB no longer contains the needed evidence. Recent work has shown that such failures are common: UAEval4RAG~\cite{peng2025uaeval4rag} highlights unsupported queries as a first-class evaluation target, while FreshLLMs~\cite{vu2023freshllms} and LiveSearchBench~\cite{zhou2025livesearchbench} show that temporally novel knowledge remains hard even with retrieval.

A common response is to keep the KB \emph{static} and improve only query-time behavior via correction, gating, or on-demand search~\cite{yan2024crag,asai2023selfrag,vu2023freshllms,kasai2024realtime}. That is useful for the current query, but it does not improve the persistent KB itself. In enterprise deployments, this is often insufficient: systems operate under strict governance constraints, latency bounds, and storage budgets, forcing them to maintain a persistent, budget-limited KB that must be improved over time rather than searching the live web per query~\cite{jin2024ragcache,comrag2025,siriwardhana2023improving}.

Prior literature on dynamic RAG mostly interprets ``updating'' as correcting outdated facts while the user queries remain structurally stable. Recent benchmarks such as HoH~\cite{hoh2025} and DynaQuest~\cite{dynaquest2025} overwhelmingly evaluate scenarios where the underlying world state shifts (i.e., temporal knowledge change). However, a distinct and equally critical, yet largely unexplored, operational challenge is \emph{query distribution drift}~\cite{gama2014survey}---where the source facts in the world remain static, but the streaming user demand shifts suddenly or gradually from popular ``head'' topics to previously unindexed ``long-tail'' topics.

How do existing persistent KB maintenance systems handle query distribution drift? Current strategies are fundamentally \emph{supply-triggered} or rely on delayed human feedback. Index-level incremental methods (e.g., LightRAG~\cite{guo2024lightrag}, HippoRAG~2~\cite{guti2025hipporag2}) integrate new documents as they arrive; editing methods (e.g., RECIPE~\cite{chen2024recipe}, Zero-RAG~\cite{zero-rag2025}) prune or correct existing facts; temporal caching methods (e.g., StreamingRAG~\cite{wu2025streamingrag}) optimize retention based on recency; and delayed log-driven methods (\emph{LogDrivenArrival}) append knowledge based on batched historical user feedback. Following shift construction methodologies established by MS-Shift~\cite{lupart2023ms} and TCR~\cite{li2024tcr}, we construct a controlled query distribution drift over streaming windows. We find that these paradigms suffer a catastrophic KB coverage collapse under fixed read-write budgets (Figure~\ref{fig:motivation-recall}). Providing supply-side methods with higher write budgets only injects noise, and log-driven methods are structurally too slow to adapt. Because they lack an immediate, precise demand signal from the query failures themselves, they replace documents arbitrarily and barely outperform a completely frozen Static baseline.

To test whether query failures carry a useful immediate \emph{demand signal} once the query stream contains reusable supporting-fact structure, we evaluate \emph{QueryDrivenCluster}, a fair post-evaluation persistent writer that updates the KB only after each window has been scored. The mechanism is intentionally minimal: a single decayed per-document demand statistic accumulated from failing-query top-K bundles, a single decayed per-document serve statistic from successful retrievals, a Bayesian serve prior on the initial KB, and one admission gate that swaps a candidate $c$ for an evictable $e$ iff $\mathrm{demand}[c] > \mathrm{serve}[e] + \mathrm{demand}[e]$. There is no drift detection, no regime branching, and no separate write cap. We now report the 100$\times$50 single-hop runs in Motivation~1 using HotpotQA's comparison subset as the main simple direct-evidence case. Here the needed evidence is directly named, and QueryDrivenCluster is the strongest persistent writer under both drift modes (H2 Coverage / Recall@5 = 30.5 / 28.3 sudden, 34.8 / 31.0 gradual). This is the live Motivation~1 figure pair. We keep a scale-matched SQuAD~v1.1~\cite{rajpurkar2016squad} probe with target $n_{source}=30000$, realized pool 20{,}958, and KB budget 5{,}000 only as a supplementary stress check in the Motivation~1 README: it remains clearly positive under sudden drift, but its long gradual run mixes the demand-signal question with a separate capacity-allocation / eviction effect. Motivation~1 therefore supports the following main-text claim: immediate query-demand signal is genuinely useful in simple direct-evidence regimes under both sudden and gradual drift, while the residual long-stream single-hop failure mode appears when capacity allocation starts to dominate.

Multi-hop drift is qualitatively different even when the construction is identical to the single-hop case. Relative to the simple positive cases above, the remaining gap in Motivation~2 is mainly a complex bridge / bundle acquisition gap: a failed multi-hop query rarely tells the persistent writer which reusable bridge evidence should be written for future queries. Under the current 100$\times$50 runs, MuSiQue is the only clean QueryDrivenCluster win (H2 Recall@5 = 22.7 sudden / 28.2 gradual), whereas HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA still favor KnowledgeEdit or LogDrivenArrival. OnDemandFetch remains far higher, which means the evidence is usually retrievable; the missing capability is bridge-aware, fetch-assisted persistent admission under low cross-query reuse.

The two motivation experiments therefore support a narrower claim than a universal QueryDrivenCluster win: query-demand signal is effective in simple direct-evidence regimes, while the remaining single-hop gap is capacity allocation under long gradual streams and the main full multi-hop gap is bridge / bundle acquisition under low reuse together with the lack of a fetch-to-persist handoff. We keep the empirical diagnosis in the main text and move the concrete algorithm design directions to \texttt{DESIGN\_DIRECTIONS.md}.
