\section{Introduction}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has substantially improved large language models (LLMs) on knowledge-intensive tasks~\cite{lewis2020rag}. In real deployments, however, the central difficulty is not whether retrieval helps in general, but how a system should respond when its current evidence is insufficient. Recent work has established that these failures are common. UAEval4RAG~\cite{peng2025uaeval4rag} shows that unsupported queries must be evaluated explicitly rather than absorbed into aggregate accuracy. FreshLLMs~\cite{vu2023freshllms} and LiveSearchBench~\cite{zhou2025livesearchbench} show that temporally novel knowledge remains difficult even with retrieval.

A well-studied line of work responds to these failures by keeping the KB \emph{static} while improving what happens at query time: retrieval-time correction, gating, and on-demand external search~\cite{yan2024crag,asai2023selfrag,vu2023freshllms,kasai2024realtime}. These methods are effective for the current query, but they leave the persistent KB unchanged. In many production deployments this is not sufficient. Systems with bounded storage, constrained or disallowed external lookup, latency budgets, or governance requirements must maintain a persistent, budget-limited KB and improve it over time. We study this well-motivated but under-solved operational regime.

Prior work on persistent KB maintenance has investigated three directions. Index-level incremental methods such as LightRAG~\cite{guo2024lightrag} and HippoRAG~2~\cite{guti2025hipporag2} optimize how newly arriving content is integrated into graph memory or index structures. Data-centric editing and pruning methods such as RECIPE~\cite{chen2024recipe} and Zero-RAG~\cite{zero-rag2025} improve the accuracy or compactness of existing knowledge. Temporal memory and caching methods such as ComRAG~\cite{comrag2025}, RAGCache~\cite{jin2024ragcache}, and StreamingRAG~\cite{wu2025streamingrag} optimize retention and reuse efficiency of admitted content. These are genuine contributions, but they share a structural commitment that limits their scope: all three directions are \emph{supply-triggered}. Index methods fire when a document arrives; editing methods operate on what is already in the corpus; caching methods promote or demote content based on recency or frequency of access. None uses the query stream itself---and specifically, queries whose supporting evidence is absent from the current KB---as the primary signal for deciding what new knowledge to acquire from an external pool under a fixed budget. This gap has measurable consequences. CRAG~\cite{yan2024crag} documents that real-world RAG queries span a long tail of low-popularity entities and temporally shifting topics that supply-driven indexing cannot track. UAEval4RAG~\cite{peng2025uaeval4rag} shows that queries lacking KB support produce systematic false answers unless the system explicitly acquires supporting evidence or abstains. Together they identify a decision that existing persistent KB methods do not make: \emph{when a support failure is observed in the query stream, should the system update the KB to fix it, and if so, with what?}

A natural solution is to treat each observed support failure as a selective acquisition trigger. The failure identifies a concrete knowledge gap; the system then decides whether that gap is correctable from an external document pool and whether it recurs often enough to justify consuming budget. We formalize this framing as \emph{budgeted query-driven KB evolution} and ask: given a stream of queries where support failures are observable, which missing knowledge is worth admitting under a fixed KB budget, and which failures indicate a genuinely unanswerable request that no update should chase?

\subsection{Motivation 1: Query-Demand Signal Is Effective on Single-Hop Queries}
\label{sec:motivation-single-hop}

Before examining complex multi-hop benchmarks, we first verify that the
query-driven update signal is \emph{per se} informative---ruling out the
hypothesis that it is simply too noisy to be useful.

\paragraph{Setup.}
We extract the \textbf{comparison} subset from HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA~\cite{ho2020constructing}.
Comparison questions (e.g.,~``Which film was released earlier, \emph{A} or \emph{B}?'')
name both target entities explicitly, so both supporting-fact documents can be retrieved
from the question alone in a single retrieval step---making this functionally
a \emph{single-hop} task.
We run the same 8-strategy suite (§\ref{sec:motivation-experiment}) under
sudden drift (50 windows, pool $\approx$ 20k--51k docs, KB $\approx$ 10\% of pool).

\paragraph{Metric: KB Coverage.}
We measure \emph{KB Coverage per window}---the fraction of a window's
ground-truth supporting-fact documents that are currently held in the KB:
\[
  \mathrm{Cov}(w) = \frac{|\mathrm{KB}_w \cap \mathrm{SF\text{-}docs}(w)|}{|\mathrm{SF\text{-}docs}(w)|}
\]
This metric is retriever-agnostic (it asks whether the right evidence is
\emph{held}, not whether the retriever \emph{finds} it), making it a clean
measure of KB content selection quality.
It is equivalent to the \emph{context recall} metric of RAGAS~\cite{es2023ragas}
and the retrieval-accuracy notion of DPR~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr}.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:motivation1-results} shows H1 and H2 KB Coverage.
Under sudden drift, QueryDriven achieves \textbf{22.4\%} H2 coverage on
HotpotQA comparison (+13.2pp over Static) and \textbf{42.7\%} on 2Wiki
comparison (+16.2pp over Static), while OnDemandFetch is comparable at
22.3\% and 33.4\% respectively.
Both easily outperform DocArrival ($\approx$9\%/27\%) and Static
($\approx$9\%/27\%).
Oracle achieves 100\% by construction.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
On single-hop comparison questions, query-demand signals \emph{do} work:
QueryDriven achieves 2.4$\times$ the H2 coverage of Static on HotpotQA and
1.6$\times$ on 2Wiki.
The performance ceiling (gap to Oracle) on multi-hop datasets is therefore
\emph{not} because the update signal is intrinsically noisy---it is because
retrieving the right \emph{multi-hop chain} of documents requires structural
graph reasoning beyond dense embedding search.
This motivates the architecture explored in Motivation~2 below.

\subsection{Motivation 2: All Existing Paradigms Fail Under Multi-Hop Query Distribution Drift}
\label{sec:motivation-experiment}

% ── 实验设计 ──

To verify that this gap is real and measurable, we design a controlled experiment across three standard multi-hop QA benchmarks—\textbf{HotpotQA}~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa}, \textbf{2WikiMultihopQA}~\cite{ho2020constructing}, and \textbf{MuSiQue}~\cite{trivedi2022musique}—each requiring retrieval of multiple supporting-fact documents per query. These three datasets are also the primary benchmarks used by HippoRAG~2~\cite{guti2025hipporag2}, making them directly relevant to the state-of-the-art continual RAG evaluation.

\paragraph{Setup.}
For each dataset, we maintain a budget-constrained KB holding approximately 10\% of the document pool (Table~\ref{tab:motivation-setup}). We embed all documents and queries with \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} and cluster queries into 8 groups via $K$-Means. The top 3 clusters form the ``head'' distribution; the remaining 5 form the ``tail.'' We construct a query stream of 20 windows: the first 10 (H1) draw 97\% head / 3\% tail queries, and the last 10 (H2) reverse to 3\% head / 97\% tail. The initial KB is biased toward head topics via centroid proximity. This setup simulates a common real-world scenario: a RAG system tuned for popular queries suddenly faces a shift toward long-tail topics.

\paragraph{Strategies.}
We compare five KB update paradigms plus an oracle upper bound:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Static}: no KB update (lower bound).
    \item \textbf{DocArrival}: document-arrival-driven update (HippoRAG/LightRAG paradigm). New documents arrive randomly from the pool; if a new document is highly similar to a KB entry ($>$0.7), it replaces it; if very dissimilar ($<$0.3), it evicts the stalest entry.
    \item \textbf{DocArrival-Heavy}: same paradigm with 2.5$\times$ operation budget, cost-matched to QueryDriven.
    \item \textbf{KnowledgeEdit}: knowledge-edit-driven update (RECIPE paradigm). External edit requests target random KB entries, replacing them with semantically similar alternatives.
    \item \textbf{QueryDriven}: demand-driven update. Identifies queries poorly served by the current KB (max query--KB similarity below threshold), retrieves candidate documents from the pool, and evicts least-useful KB entries. Uses adaptive scoring (auto-switching between MEAN and MAX based on embedding signal quality).
    \item \textbf{Oracle}: upper bound. At the H1$\to$H2 transition, rebuilds the KB with perfect knowledge of all H2 supporting facts. This is not a realistic strategy but establishes the ceiling achievable with ideal content selection under the same KB budget.
\end{enumerate}

% ── 分析 ──

\paragraph{Finding 1: Catastrophic recall collapse is universal.}
Across all three datasets and all strategies except Oracle, Recall@5 drops by 12--26 percentage points from H1 to H2. Even the Static baseline drops from 21--26\% to near zero, confirming that the initial KB provides essentially no coverage for the shifted distribution. This is not a model quality problem---the same embedding model achieves 20+\% Recall@5 in H1---but a \emph{knowledge inventory mismatch}: the KB simply does not contain the evidence needed for the new query distribution.

\paragraph{Finding 2: Supply-side paradigms lack demand signals by design.}
DocArrival and KnowledgeEdit improve H2 Recall@5 by at most 3.9 and 3.0 percentage points over Static, respectively. Even DocArrival-Heavy with 2.5$\times$ budget reaches only 3.9\% (HotpotQA), 2.8\% (2Wiki), and 5.1\% (MuSiQue) in H2. The comparison with QueryDriven is intentionally asymmetric: QueryDriven leverages query failure signals that DocArrival and KnowledgeEdit \emph{cannot access by design}. This is precisely the point---existing RAG KB maintenance systems (HippoRAG, RECIPE) trigger updates from the supply side (document arrival, edit requests) and have no architectural pathway to discover \emph{what knowledge the current queries need}. The performance gap demonstrates that this missing demand signal is the primary bottleneck, not algorithm sophistication.

\paragraph{Finding 3: Demand-driven signals help but face structural limits.}
QueryDriven achieves the highest H2 Recall@5 among realistic methods on two of three datasets (7.5\% on HotpotQA, 8.8\% on MuSiQue), demonstrating that query failure signals are a strictly better update trigger than supply-side signals. However, even the best realistic method recovers only 30--42\% of pre-drift performance. Three structural factors explain this ceiling:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Adaptation latency}: QueryDriven must \emph{observe} failing queries before it can react; the first 2--3 windows of H2 are effectively wasted.
    \item \textbf{Replacement budget}: At 60 swaps per window, fully rebuilding a 1{,}000-doc KB takes $\sim$17 windows---longer than the 10-window H2 period.
    \item \textbf{Content selection precision}: With a lightweight encoder, the system cannot reliably identify which pool documents will actually serve as supporting facts for failing queries.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Finding 4: The Oracle gap reveals the true bottleneck.}
The Oracle upper bound---which rebuilds the KB with perfect knowledge of H2 supporting facts---achieves 74.5\% (HotpotQA), 60.9\% (2Wiki), and 55.1\% (MuSiQue) Recall@5 in H2, \emph{far exceeding} pre-drift performance. The gap between QueryDriven and Oracle is striking: 67.0pp on HotpotQA, 58.2pp on 2Wiki, and 46.3pp on MuSiQue. This demonstrates that the KB budget is \emph{not} the bottleneck---the same 1{,}000-document budget supports 74.5\% recall when filled with the right content. The bottleneck is \emph{content selection precision}: QueryDriven uses 731 swaps (HotpotQA) compared to Oracle's 995, yet achieves only 7.5\% vs.\ 74.5\%. The problem is not how many documents are replaced, but \emph{which} documents are selected. This motivates a system that goes beyond simple embedding-space heuristics to incorporate deeper semantic understanding of what knowledge the query stream needs.

\paragraph{Finding 5: Progressive recovery reveals the learning dynamic.}
Analyzing per-window Recall@5 trajectories reveals a key distinction (Figure~\ref{fig:motivation-recall}). Supply-side strategies show flat H2 curves: DocArrival-Heavy's per-window R@5 fluctuates around 3--6\% throughout H2 with no upward trend. In contrast, QueryDriven shows a clear learning ramp: from 1--3\% in early H2 windows to 8--12\% in late H2 windows. This confirms that demand-driven signals enable progressive adaptation, but the adaptation is too slow and imprecise to close the gap to Oracle.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
This experiment establishes four facts that motivate our work:
(1)~query distribution drift causes catastrophic and universal recall collapse in budget-constrained RAG systems;
(2)~existing supply-side paradigms (document arrival, knowledge editing) cannot compensate because they lack a mechanism to \emph{discover} what knowledge the shifted queries need;
(3)~demand-driven approaches significantly outperform supply-side methods, confirming that query failure signals are the correct update trigger;
(4)~the Oracle gap (55--75\% vs.\ 3--9\%) proves that the KB budget is sufficient---the bottleneck is content selection precision. Simple embedding-space heuristics cannot identify the right documents to acquire. These findings motivate a system that combines demand-driven KB evolution with deeper semantic understanding of knowledge gaps.
