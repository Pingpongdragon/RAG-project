\section{Introduction}

\paragraph{Setting.} As autonomous agents evolve from transient chat-bots to long-lived cognitive entities, their architectures have diverged into dual layers: private local memory and shared macroscopic knowledge. The community has largely focused on the semantic management of local memory~\cite{hu2025memory,weng2025amem}, yet the management of the shared knowledge layer—the agents' collective gateway to the world—remains an underserved engineering challenge. In latency-sensitive deployments, this layer is physically realized as a tiered vector index~\cite{jayaram2019diskann,malkov2018efficient}, where a small, high-velocity hot-tier must cache the most relevant "knowledge working set" in RAM. Crucially, this working set is not static; it is subject to the dynamic drift of multi-user collective intent. When the hot tier fails to proactively adapt to these demand shifts, cache misses shift repeated demand to the cold tier, increasing tail retrieval cost, storage pressure, and serving variance across concurrent users and repeated agent-loop iterations.

\paragraph{The Prevalence of Query Distribution Drift} In real-world deployments, the workload of a shared knowledge base is rarely stationary. User-generated queries exhibit significant distributional drift over time, driven by the dynamic nature of collective attention~\cite{yang2025murr}. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:motivation0}, monthly topic mixtures in 32.5K WildChat-1M~\cite{zhao2024wildchat} ChatGPT conversations fluctuate substantially across months, reflecting a macroscopic drift in user interest. Similarly, Google Trends data shows that user focus across major LLM application domains is continuously reweighted over a multi-year horizon. In dynamic production environments (e.g., daily news or social media streams), this query demand drift is heavily entangled with corpus updates. However, crucially, this query-side drift persists \emph{even} when the underlying corpus is completely fixed—such as in legal archives, API documentation, or medical guidelines—where the active working set is dictated purely by evolving user intent rather than changes in the data itself. Consequently, query distribution drift represents a universal, first-class challenge in long-running agent environments, as it constantly reshapes the demand for the limited capacity of the hot tier across both continuously evolving streams and static knowledge archives.


\paragraph{A signal-source audit of existing policies.} Figure~\ref{fig:streamingqa} audits existing cache-management families on StreamingQA~\cite{liska2022streamingqa}, a 14-year news QA workload whose five historical eras (R1--R5) induce natural demand drift. We use StreamingQA as a \emph{single-hop calibration} rather than as the main showcase: when each query usually points to its required evidence directly, the access pattern is a reasonable proxy for future use. Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation1} then isolates static-corpus multi-hop settings where this approximation breaks. We organize the related work by signal source and use the audit to identify when each signal is sufficient, and when it is not.

(\textbf{i})~\emph{Document-centric signals.} Temporal RAG and TTL-style caches~\cite{hoh2025,dynaquest2025} rely on publication timestamps. While a timestamp identifies recent eras, it lacks the \emph{intra-era resolution} to select the specific 400 slots among thousands of co-dated candidates. Our \textsc{Recency-TTL} oracle confirms this: even with oracle access to publication years, post-R1 Recall@5 collapses to near zero in later eras (Fig~\ref{fig:streamingqa}a). Similarly, streaming ANN or graph updates~\cite{guo2024lightrag,guti2025hipporag2} focus on incremental ingestion (\textsc{IndexGrowth}); once the hot tier is saturated, they lack a principled eviction logic and effectively track the frozen \textsc{Static} baseline. 

(\textbf{ii})~\emph{Semantic response and serving-level caches.} GPTCache~\cite{bang2023gptcache} explicitly performs semantic caching: it embeds incoming LLM queries, searches a vector store for similar cached requests, and reuses a stored response when the similarity evaluator accepts the match. RAGCache~\cite{jin2024ragcache} similarly optimizes the serving stack through reusable RAG computation. These systems are important but complementary to our setting. Their cache unit is a response, request, or intermediate serving state; our unit is an evidence document in a bounded RAM hot tier. Thus, semantic response reuse can reduce repeated LLM calls, but it does not decide which cold-corpus documents should be proactively admitted into the shared vector index before future retrieval requests arrive. 

(\textbf{iii})~\emph{Traditional frequency and recency caches.} Standard system policies like \textsc{LRU} or its modern frequency-aware variant \textsc{TinyLFU} operate on access history. We do not argue that these policies are strawmen. On StreamingQA, where most questions are single-hop, \textsc{LRU} is already a strong policy: it reaches $28.8\%$ Recall@5 because the documents recently accessed by one query are often the documents needed by nearby future queries. SemFlow is slightly lower on this calibration workload ($27.1\%$), showing that semantic admission is viable but not the main source of gain. The limitation appears only as the task structure becomes less direct: when the useful evidence is visible to the query embedding, simple query-side retrieval and access history are often enough; when the useful evidence is hidden behind an intermediate entity, both signals become insufficient. This motivates the static multi-hop diagnosis in Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation1}.

(\textbf{iv})~\emph{Agent-centric and reactive frameworks.} Conversational memory (e.g., MemGPT~\cite{packer2023memgpt}) manages per-user session context, which does not reflect the macroscopic drift of a shared corpus. Meanwhile, evaluation-based retrieval correction~\cite{yan2024crag,peng2025uaeval4rag} can detect failures but lacks a prescriptive admission logic. The default fallback, \textsc{On-Demand Fetch}, recovers recall but shifts serving work from the hot tier to repeated cold-tier access. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:streamingqa}b, \textsc{On-Demand} triggers an average of \textbf{29 logical cold-tier fetches per query} (totaling over 145K fetches across the stream). We interpret these fetches as a hardware-agnostic proxy for cold-tier pressure rather than a direct SSD latency measurement. The architectural issue is that purely reactive retrieval repeatedly pays cold-tier cost for demand that a better admission policy could make resident. Crucially, Fig.~\ref{fig:streamingqa}c shows that updating strategies maintain acceptable algorithmic overhead (4--10 ms per window in CPU simulation), confirming that the bottleneck is the \emph{signal} driving admission, not the update computation itself.


\paragraph{SemFlow: Shifting from ID history to spatial query demand.} SemFlow is not introduced as a universal replacement for access-history caches: on StreamingQA, \textsc{LRU}'s $28.8\%$ Recall@5 is slightly higher than SemFlow's $27.1\%$ because single-hop access history already approximates future use. Instead, SemFlow isolates a different signal source. On each cache miss, it drives demand not only to the single nearest pool document, but propagates weighted demand across the top-$k$ semantic neighbors of the unsatisfied query—each neighbor accrues demand proportional to its cosine similarity to the query, scaled by a neighborhood factor $\gamma$. A cold document is admitted only when its accumulated demand exceeds the eviction utility of its weakest resident competitor, penalized for intra-KB redundancy. This soft neighborhood propagation allows SemFlow to co-admit semantically co-occurring documents—a prerequisite for answering comparison multi-hop questions where two distinct entities must be simultaneously resident.

\paragraph{Deconstructing the multi-hop bottleneck: Direct vs. bridge evidence.} 
Empirical analysis reveals that traditional access-history caching remains highly competitive under certain conditions. In the HotpotQA direct-evidence setting, where query embeddings naturally align with the target evidence regions, \textsc{LRU} maintains a respectable $44.6\%$ Recall@5. \textsc{SemFlow} elevates this baseline to $54.3\%$ (\textbf{+9.7 pp}; Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation1}a) because its \textbf{soft demand propagation} distributes demand across top-$k$ semantic neighbors rather than allocating all credit to a single winner-take-all retrieval target. While this demonstrates that semantic neighborhood signals successfully densify relevant cache regions, direct-evidence retrieval represents a relatively benign regime where the query shares explicit semantic overlap with the target documents.

Conversely, bridge-style multi-hop retrieval introduces a severe structural bottleneck that exposes the boundary of purely query-driven cache admission. In 2WikiMultihopQA, the required evidence chain follows a disjoint trajectory $Q \rightarrow A \rightarrow B$: the query explicitly references entity $A$, but the crucial bridging document for entity $B$ is only identifiable after resolving $A$'s content. Because document $B$ lacks semantic proximity to the initial query $Q$ in the embedding space, direct query-embedding lookup has no reliable vector path to its location. Consequently, \textsc{SemFlow} yields a more modest \textbf{+7.3 pp} improvement over \textsc{LRU}, leaving a substantial \textbf{21 pp} deficit relative to the Oracle ceiling (Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation1}b). This performance gap directly motivates the design of \textsc{[Method]}'s \textbf{entity-chained prefetch} mechanism. Upon admitting or retrieving the primary document $A$, the system parses its content to extract the linked entity $B$, pre-emptively warming the hot tier with $B$'s evidence before subsequent reasoning steps fail. Complex topological structures, including extended chains ($A\!\rightarrow\!B\!\rightarrow\!C$), temporally drifting or implicit bridges, and cold-start entities, are beyond the primary scope of this work and are addressed as formal limitations.

\paragraph{Contributions.} Our core contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item We formalize \emph{query distribution drift} as the primary challenge for shared KB hot-tier admission, distinct from corpus-side freshness.
    \item We provide a three-level empirical diagnosis: single-hop StreamingQA where access history is already competitive, direct-evidence HotpotQA where semantic propagation adds additional gains, and bridge multi-hop where query-only signals expose a 21 pp Oracle gap.
    \item We propose \textbf{SemFlow}, a semantic admission module that replaces ID-only recency with query-document demand propagation, and use its bridge failure to motivate \textsc{[Method]}, an entity-chained prefetch extension for content-driven hot-tier refresh.
\end{itemize}
