\section{Introduction}

LLM-based agent systems maintain two distinct kinds of memory: a \emph{personal} episodic layer (conversation histories, task state, and user preferences) and a \emph{shared} factual knowledge base accessed through retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Recent work has made the episodic layer genuinely dynamic: MemGPT~\cite{packer2023memgpt}, MemoryBank~\cite{zhong2023memorybank}, and A-MEM~\cite{weng2025amem} demonstrate agents that continuously reorganize and update their own memories in response to interaction. The shared factual knowledge base, however, remains almost universally static---pre-indexed at deployment time and never updated in response to how users actually query it. This asymmetry is benign when demand is stable, but in long-running multi-user deployments the query distribution over the shared KB evolves: early ``head'' topics give way to ``long-tail'' demands the frozen corpus cannot support~\cite{vu2023freshllms,zhou2025livesearchbench,peng2025uaeval4rag}. Making the RAG knowledge base itself \emph{dynamic}---responsive to query distribution drift rather than only to personal episodic history---is the natural extension of the agent-memory agenda.

A common response is to keep the KB \emph{static} and improve only query-time behavior via correction, gating, or on-demand search~\cite{yan2024crag,asai2023selfrag,vu2023freshllms,kasai2024realtime}. That is useful for the current query, but it does not improve the persistent KB itself.

In practice, per-query on-demand retrieval is often precluded by governance constraints, latency bounds, and storage budgets, making it necessary to maintain a persistent, budget-limited KB that improves over time~\cite{jin2024ragcache,comrag2025,siriwardhana2023improving}. The challenge is therefore not just \emph{how} to retrieve, but \emph{what} to persist.

Prior literature on dynamic RAG mostly interprets ``updating'' as correcting outdated facts while the user queries remain structurally stable. Recent benchmarks such as HoH~\cite{hoh2025} and DynaQuest~\cite{dynaquest2025} overwhelmingly evaluate scenarios where the underlying world state shifts (i.e., temporal knowledge change). However, a distinct and equally critical, yet largely unexplored, operational challenge is \emph{query distribution drift}. Classical concept-drift surveys distinguish several shift patterns, including sudden, gradual, incremental, and recurring drift~\cite{gama2014survey}. In our setting, the source facts in the world remain static, but the streaming user demand can evolve through these modes as it moves from popular ``head'' topics toward previously unindexed ``long-tail'' topics.

How do existing persistent KB maintenance systems handle query distribution drift? Current strategies are fundamentally \emph{supply-triggered} or rely on delayed human feedback. Index-level incremental methods (e.g., LightRAG~\cite{guo2024lightrag}, HippoRAG~2~\cite{guti2025hipporag2}) integrate new documents as they arrive; editing methods (e.g., RECIPE~\cite{chen2024recipe}, Zero-RAG~\cite{zero-rag2025}) prune or correct existing facts; temporal caching methods (e.g., StreamingRAG~\cite{wu2025streamingrag}) optimize retention based on recency; and delayed log-driven methods (\emph{LogDrivenArrival}) append knowledge based on batched historical user feedback. Following the classical taxonomy of drift modes~\cite{gama2014survey} together with shift-construction methodologies established by MS-Shift~\cite{lupart2023ms} and TCR~\cite{li2024tcr}, we construct controlled query shifts over streaming windows, with our experiments focusing on the sudden and gradual cases. We find that these paradigms suffer a catastrophic KB coverage collapse under fixed read-write budgets (Figure~\ref{fig:motivation1}). Providing supply-side methods with higher write budgets only injects noise, and log-driven methods are structurally too slow to adapt. Because they lack an immediate, precise demand signal from the query failures themselves, they replace documents arbitrarily and barely outperform a completely frozen Static baseline.

Our first diagnostic study focuses on a \emph{simple direct-evidence regime}: unsupported queries directly name the missing evidence, and that evidence is reusable across future queries, as in a RAG-augmented agent answering factual questions over entities already present in its broader knowledge pool. This is the cleanest setting in which query failures can guide persistent KB updates. Figure~\ref{fig:motivation1} shows that, in this simple regime, immediate query-demand signals are genuinely useful under both sudden and gradual drift. It also clarifies why not every superficially similar dataset behaves the same. When the pool contains almost no distractors, as in TriviaQA-style entity-page pools, QueryDrivenCluster can still beat Static, but the gap to the best offline baseline shrinks because even blind refresh can land on useful documents by chance. The remaining weakness in longer gradual streams is therefore not the absence of a useful signal, but a budget-allocation problem: the system must preserve high-reuse evidence while still reserving capacity for newly emerging tail knowledge.

Our second diagnostic study should likewise be read as a \emph{signal-and-acquisition} problem, not merely as ``multi-hop is harder.'' In bridge-required settings, the query alone often provides a weakly aligned signal for the reusable evidence that should be persisted: a failed query reveals that the KB is insufficient, but does not directly identify which intermediate bridge document or evidence bundle should be written for future reuse. Figure~\ref{fig:motivation2} shows that QueryDrivenCluster remains the strongest \emph{persistent writer} across all datasets, which means the persistent demand signal is still real. The large remaining gap is specifically to on-demand fetching. From an agent perspective, OnDemandFetch is therefore not just a competing baseline; it is the \emph{online acquisition channel} that surfaces candidate evidence outside the current persistent KB. The unresolved problem is how to convert those transient on-demand discoveries---retrieved bridge documents, successful contexts, and other retrieval traces---into persistent KB admissions for future reuse.

These two diagnostic studies therefore point to a concrete algorithmic agenda rather than a universal QueryDrivenCluster win. An effective dynamic KB maintenance algorithm must address four coupled challenges. \textbf{First}, it must transform unsupported queries into a reliable demand signal for persistent KB updates, rather than relying solely on supply-triggered document arrivals or delayed feedback. \textbf{Second}, it must determine when that signal is recurrent and stable enough to justify persistence, so that adaptation is triggered neither too early nor too late. \textbf{Third}, it must allocate a fixed KB budget in a reuse-aware manner, preserving high-value evidence while still admitting newly emerging tail knowledge under gradual drift. \textbf{Fourth}, it must support \emph{fetch-to-persist conversion}: when agent-side on-demand retrieval uncovers useful evidence that the query alone did not localize, the system must decide which retrieved documents or evidence bundles should be admitted into the long-lived KB for future reuse.

Toward this end, we develop a query-driven dynamic KB maintenance framework that uses unsupported queries as update signals and integrates demand-aware admission, agent-side acquisition signals, fetch-to-persist handoff, and budget-aware eviction. \textbf{In summary, our main contributions are as follows:}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item We formalize \emph{query distribution drift} as a distinct challenge for persistent RAG systems under fixed KB budgets---including the shared knowledge modules of RAG-augmented agent deployments---and show that it is not adequately captured by prior settings centered on temporal fact updates alone.
    \item We introduce a quantitative drift-reconstruction protocol for persistent RAG streams that localizes the timing of distribution shift over streaming windows, enabling controlled evaluation of when adaptation should be triggered under sudden and gradual regimes.
    \item We propose a query-driven dynamic KB maintenance framework that directly targets the dominant failure modes exposed by these diagnostic studies, namely mistimed adaptation, capacity allocation under long gradual drift, and fetch-to-persist admission when query-only signals under-specify reusable evidence.
    \item We provide an empirical diagnosis of why existing supply-triggered, recency-driven, or lagged-update strategies fail in this regime, characterize the simple settings in which immediate query-demand signals are sufficient, and show when additional agent-side acquisition and evidence-aware admission mechanisms become necessary.
\end{itemize}
